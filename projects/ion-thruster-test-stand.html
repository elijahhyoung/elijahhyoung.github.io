MIT 16.522 - Electric Propulsion

Large Area Cusped-Field Space Ramjet

A 15-person team project designing, building, and testing an air-breathing electric propulsion (ABEP) thruster: a concept that collects ambient atmosphere in very low Earth orbit, ionizes it, and accelerates it out the back to counteract drag, instead of carrying propellant onboard. I worked on the CAD grid design and material selection, machining and deburring frame components, soldering the high-voltage wiring, running test configurations, degassing the vacuum chamber, and building a quick repair test stand between runs.

Domain
Electric propulsion
My role
Grid design, fabrication, and testing
Team
15 members, MIT AeroAstro
Outputs
Designed, built, and tested the ramjet

Challenge

Satellites in very low Earth orbit lose altitude fast to atmospheric drag, and carrying enough propellant to fight that limits payload mass and mission life. The idea behind an air-breathing thruster is to skip the onboard propellant entirely: collect the thin atmosphere the satellite is already flying through, ionize it, and accelerate it out the back for thrust. Our job was to design, build, and test whether a large-area cusped-field magnetic design could actually do that, not just simulate it.

  • 16 magnet pairs plus a center pair needed precise, repeatable cusp geometry.
  • Grids needed jackscrew adjustment up to 2" without rebuilding the assembly.
  • The full assembly had to fit the vacuum chamber via a "drop-in" mount.
  • 15 people on one thruster meant parallel work on magnets, grids, and mounting.

Approach & Key Decisions

The photos above trace the actual build: CAD, then assembled hardware, then the plasma test that proved it worked. The magnetic circuit uses 16 magnet pairs around the circumference plus one center pair, each pair held in a threaded tube with a Teflon sleeve for electrical isolation. The mounting design went through a "drop-in" iteration so the base and thruster could be positioned in the vacuum chamber one at a time, which maximized how large a thruster diameter we could fit. The grids sit on jackscrews for independent adjustment, up to about 2" of separation, so the electric field geometry could be tuned without rebuilding the assembly.

My part of that design was the CAD grid design and material selection, plus assisting with machining and deburring the frame components. Mounting and grid iteration were shared work across the structures team.

Fabrication & Testing

Machining and deburring frame components, soldering the high-voltage wiring, and assembling and aligning the thruster hardware onto the test stand plate. Between test runs I also built a quick repair test stand so the team could troubleshoot hardware issues without tying up the main vacuum chamber setup.

From first plasma, the team ran dozens of configurations (grounded, floating, and biased thruster structure, plus varying grid spacing and vacuum conditions) chasing higher thrust and efficiency. I was involved in running those configurations and degassing the chamber between them.

Results

Confirmed net thrust: 13.6 µN, specific impulse of 7,000 s, and 7.14% efficiency in the best-performing (floating) configuration.

The plasma photos and video above are from first-plasma testing, the first point where the thruster proved it could ionize and accelerate propellant at all: 3.6 µN of thrust, a specific impulse of about 10,600 s, and roughly 1% efficiency. The floating configuration came out on top of every configuration tested.

Future work and improvements would include:

  • Optimizing the magnetic field and structure to improve ion utilization while maintaining electron confinement.
  • Insulating the high-voltage grid to suppress unwanted plasma formation.
