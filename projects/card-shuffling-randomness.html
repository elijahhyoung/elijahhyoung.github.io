MIT 2.671 - Measurement and Instrumentation

From Order to Chaos

Experimental research quantifying how riffle, overhand, and machine shuffling methods approach randomness. A computer vision pipeline tracked card order with ArUco fiducial markers across 210 trials and evaluated positional histograms, Adjacent-Pair Survival, and normalized Total Variation distance.

Question
How quickly do shuffle methods randomize a deck?
Methods
ArUco markers, computer vision, statistics
Trials
210
Outputs
Paper and poster
Read the paper (PDF) View full poster (PDF)

Challenge

"Shuffle the deck" means very different things depending on how you shuffle. This project turned that everyday assumption into something I could actually measure instead of just trust.

  • Had to track all 52 card positions, repeatedly, across shuffle counts k = 0 to 7.
  • Riffle, overhand, and machine shuffling needed to be compared on the same metrics.
  • Measurement had to be automated enough to make 210 trials realistic instead of a week of manual counting.

Approach & Metrics

I built a computer vision pipeline around ArUco fiducial markers so each card could be identified from an overhead photo without me hand-logging deck orders. That gave me clean data to run local and global randomness metrics on.

  • Adjacent-Pair Survival measured how many originally neighboring card pairs remained adjacent after shuffling.
  • Normalized Total Variation distance measured how close card-position distributions were to a uniform random baseline.
  • Positional histograms visualized card movement across repeated shuffles.

Results

Human riffle edges out machine shuffling; both are miles ahead of overhand. Riffle at least four times for a fair shuffle.

Riffle and machine shuffles were comparable, with human riffles mixing slightly faster (Adjacent-Pair Survival rate of 1.49, Total Variation decay of 0.53) than the machine (APS rate of 1.56, TV decay of 0.40). Overhand shuffling was dramatically slower on both metrics (APS rate of 0.55, TV decay of 0.23), confirming it's a poor way to actually randomize a deck. Both riffle and machine shuffling reach full randomness in around seven shuffles; overhand still isn't there after seven. It turns out you can measure that difference precisely with an overhead camera and some markers instead of just taking it on faith.
