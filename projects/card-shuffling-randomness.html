MIT 2.671 - Measurement and Instrumentation

From Order to Chaos

Experimental research quantifying how riffle, overhand, and machine shuffling methods approach randomness. A computer vision pipeline tracked card order with ArUco fiducial markers across 210 trials and evaluated positional histograms, Adjacent-Pair Survival, and normalized Total Variation distance.

Question
How quickly do shuffle methods randomize a deck?
Methods
ArUco markers, computer vision, statistics
Trials
210
Outputs
Paper and poster deck

Challenge

Many card games depend on fair shuffling, but different methods mix at very different rates. The project turned that everyday question into a measurable experiment.

Constraints

  • Track all 52 card positions repeatedly across shuffle counts k = 0 to 7.
  • Compare riffle, overhand, and machine shuffling with consistent metrics.
  • Automate measurement enough to make 210 trials practical.

Design Approach

A custom computer vision system used ArUco fiducial markers to identify each card from overhead photos. The resulting deck orders enabled local and global randomness metrics.

Metrics

  • Adjacent-Pair Survival measured how many originally neighboring card pairs remained adjacent after shuffling.
  • Normalized Total Variation distance measured how close card-position distributions were to a uniform random baseline.
  • Positional histograms visualized card movement across repeated shuffles.

Results

From the poster data: riffle and machine shuffles approached the theoretical baseline of two surviving pairs, with APS randomization rates of 1.49 and 1.56 per shuffle. Overhand shuffling mixed more slowly at 0.55 per shuffle. Global TV decay rates were 0.53 for riffle, 0.40 for machine, and 0.23 for overhand.

Conclusion

The poster concludes that riffle-style shuffles randomize far faster than overhand shuffles and that computer vision with ArUco tracking enabled automated local and global measurement across the trial set.
