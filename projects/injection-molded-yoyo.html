MIT 2.008 - Manufacturing

The Eye of Agamotto

A Doctor Strange-themed injection-molded yo-yo, built with a four-person team. The project connects product design, design for manufacturing, mold and tooling considerations, injection molding, process planning, and final product quality.

Process
Injection molding, thermoforming + CNC
Focus
DFM, tooling, CAD + CAM
Deliverable
50 production-quality yo-yos
Outputs
Final poster and Team Eye site

Challenge

Take a yo-yo from a product concept to an actual injection-molded part, where geometry, tooling, shrinkage, and finish all decide whether the final piece looks like the render or looks like a mistake. Our team's concept was the Eye of Agamotto, Doctor Strange's amulet: an oblong eye shape with smooth outer edges, fine engraved detail, overhanging "branch" features, and a green thermoformed centerpiece standing in for the Time Stone.

  • Geometry had to work with molding and survive post-mold assembly.
  • Tooling decisions had direct tradeoffs against manufacturability and finish quality.
  • Process planning had to turn design intent into a repeatable result.
  • Fine engraved detail was limited by the 1/16" end mill used on the ring molds, and by needing enough surface area for ejector pins.

Approach & Key Decisions

This was as much a DFM project as a product design one. The design went through three real iterations (9/17 initial, 10/12, and the 10/21 final) as manufacturing constraints pushed the geometry: channels got widened where fine detail collided with the end mill diameter and ejector-pin clearance, edges got smoothed and given a 2° draft angle so parts would release cleanly instead of sticking in the mold.

Press fit design used a commonly accepted interference value of 0.015", with correct machined dimensions confirmed through shrinkage simulation before cutting metal. In practice, the measured diameters drifted from the intended specification limits (body: 2.02"-2.04"; ring: 1.99"-2.01"), but because both mating parts shrank by roughly the same amount, the mean interference still landed close to nominal, so the press fit worked anyway.

Fabrication & Testing

Two processes: injection molding on an Arburg machine produced 50 top rings, 50 bottom rings, and 100 body parts from three aluminum molds; thermoforming (thermoformer, punch, and spray paint over an SLA-printed mold) produced 50 of the green "Time Stone" centerpieces. The manufacturing spec and press-fit distributions in the poster above are the real process data from the build, not mockups.

The ring molds were the hardest part to get right: the detailed channels caused tool breakage and rough surfaces that slowed material removal, and one groove was too narrow to machine at all, so we widened it. The bottom ring came out with an oversized press fit at first, fixed by rescaling for shrinkage. The body parts showed injection-molding burning until we dropped injection velocity to 0.5 m/s and pressure to 400 psi. The thermoformed piece was hard to center under the die-cutter, solved by adding a sharp "cliff" edge into the mold as an alignment reference.

Results

Delivered 50 complete, production-quality yo-yos through an injection molding and thermoforming workflow, matching the batch size across every part: 50 top rings, 50 bottom rings, 100 body halves, and 50 Time Stone centerpieces.

The press fit held up through a 4-foot drop test.
